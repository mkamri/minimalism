minimalism mika's ultimate beginner’s guide

let’s make this simple.

As more and more people enjoy the life-changing benefits of simplicity, our simple-living online space has gotten cluttered with opinions and arguments about the “best” way to be a minimalist.

I'm here to simplify all that information into a one-stop resource to help you launch your very own minimalist journey. I’ll also be linking some popular blogs, books and articles along the way for further reading.

defining minimalism:

  1. A minimalist is someone without excess who focuses on what matters to them.
  2. You get to decide what minimalism means for you, so long as you live intentionally.
  3. Minimalism is a personal commitment to simplicity, nothing more and nothing less. 

three steps to minimalist freedom

1: find your why

Search within yourself. What do you want from simplifying? What does your dream life look like?

2: get rid of junk

Get rid of the stuff that’s consuming your time, energy, and money. Make room for what’s truly important.

3: infinity and beyond

Discover time and energy you could have never dreamed of. Learn what your true values are. Experience the satisfaction of finally being enough.

step onefind your why

why minimalism?

If you’ve ever read a self-help book, you’ve probably heard of the importance of why-power. Having a firm grasp on why you’re doing something is crucial to sticking with it when things get tough.

Minimalism may be simple, but it isn’t easy at first. There will be moments when you question yourself, when you relapse, or when you want to give up entirely. In these difficult times, having a journal entry or a word document reminding you of your goals is paramount.

common why's:

My house is a mess.

I feel like I’m constantly cleaning, but nothing seems to stay clean for long. I feel anxious or exhausted when I come home and see the state of things. I hate doing huge loads of laundry. The dishes seem to be forever dirty. I lose things often. I want to enjoy my living space.

I keep buying things I don’t need.

I’m in debt, but for some reason I keep buying things I know I don’t need. I run out of money by the end of the month and don’t know where it goes. I keep finding excuses to go shopping and it’s affecting my relationships and my mental health. I want to heal my finances.

I’m always distracted.

I feel like my brain is jumbled up. I have trouble focusing on one thing at a time. I have so much I need to do every day and I can’t seem to find time for my personal goals. I have so many projects that I don’t end up working on any of them. I want to become productive.

take action

Write down what you are dissatisfied with in your current life. This can be anything, including things that seem unrelated to minimalism.

Use this dissatisfaction list to determine what you wish your life looked like. This could be a dream schedule (I wish my day looked like…), a mood wishlist (I wish my life was relaxing, adventurous, productive, etc), or anything else you’d like more of in your life.

Get creative! Write yourself a letter for when you get frustrated and want to give up. Draw a picture of your dream living room. Make a pinterest board of some minimalist spaces that inspire you. Do whatever you need to get yourself motivated to get started, and to keep you going through the hard days.

step twoget rid of it

Alright, you’re ready to take the plunge. How exactly do you go about getting rid of your stuff?

This initial “purge” is the most debated and complicated part of the minimalist journey. Should I keep this or give it away? What about if it was a gift? How about if it brings me joy? Knitting is my favorite hobby and I need at least fifty balls of yarn to even wake up in the morning, how about that?

"What if I need this someday?"

In the end, it’s up to you to decide what method to use to get rid of your stuff. You get to choose your guidelines on what stays and what goes. It’s your life, after all.

However, if you want to see serious effects in your life, you have to make serious changes. No one ever sat around thinking about how they should do something and ended up with life-changing results.

what to do:

  • Keep a thing that you use on a daily, weekly, or even monthly basis.
  • Keep a thing that brings you a lot of joy.
  • Keep a thing that aligns with the lifestyle you defined in step one.
  • Start by getting rid of obvious junk, then make your way up to sentimental items.
  • Set a start date and an end date for your initial big purge. Stop waiting for it to be convenient. It will never be convenient to uproot your life and start fresh.
  • Choose a method to get rid of your things, such as the KonMari method, the Minimalist game , the Packing Party, or another method that you’ve defined.

what not to do:

  • Keep a thing only because it was a gift. The gift giver will probably never notice that it’s gone, anyway.
  • Keep a thing only because it was expensive. The biggest waste is using your space on something you don’t want anymore.
  • Keep a thing "just in case". Unless you know exactly when you will use the item next, you are wasting time and space on a future you who may never exist. Just get rid of it. I promise you will only end up re-purchasing a small fraction of what you’ve given away, and the re-buy will be money well spent.

step threeinfinity and beyond

Congratulations! You’ve gotten rid of your excess. You can start to reap some of the benefits like peace of mind, more meaningful relationships, more money in your bank account and free time to spend on the things you want.

However, minimalism is not a destination. In fact, you may be left with more questions than answers. “What do I do about my family members who disagree with my new lifestyle?” “How do I tell people I don’t want gifts anymore?” “I still have the urge to buy things, what do I do?” These are just some of the questions that minimalists often face as they set off on their journeys.

Minimalism means beign intentional about how you life. It's a path that requires you to pay attention to your changing needs and circumstances. What worked before minimalism may not work for you now, and what works for you now may not be useful a year in the future.

This means that as a minimalist, you must commit to being a lifelong learner. More than counting the number of shirts you own or trying to fit all your belongings into a backpack, you have to decide that you want to keep learning about yourself. You need to keep listening to your own needs and come up with creative solutions to solve them. You need to keep reading, and stay informed. Most of all, you need to get curious about yourself and about the world around you.

Here's your first lesson: you are enough.

You don’t need more clothes, more makeup, or more toys for your more hobbies. You don’t need a bigger house, a bigger car, or a bigger paycheck. You don’t need anything you don’t already have. This is the message that you will learn by living with less. You are enough, exactly as you are.

You refuse to be a part of the cycle of desire, purchase, boredom, and more desire. Enough is enough.

Go forth and prosper, my friend. Enjoy your life.

You are free.
